# Looking Good vs Being Right Assessment

Surface and examine where social pressure causes you to optimize for appearance rather than outcomes.

## Core Principle

**Lynch**: "Most investors want to justify their own errors to keep their jobs, rather than take smart risks. They'd rather *look good* than *be right*."

**The IBM Trap**: "You'll never lose your job losing your client's money in IBM."

**The Pattern**: Social incentives overwhelmingly favor looking good. Being right requires actively resisting these incentives.

## The Assessment Framework

### Step 1: Identify the Social Calculation

| Pattern | Example | Hidden Fear |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| **Reputation protection** | "I can't propose that — too unconventional" | Being seen as weird/wrong |
| **Conformity seeking** | "I'll do what others are doing" | Standing out from the group |
| **Safe choice making** | "Let's go with the established option" | Being blamed for failure |
| **Eloquent avoidance** | "Let me explain why we need more research..." | Looking foolish if wrong |
| **Consensus waiting** | "I'll wait to see what others think first" | Having an unpopular opinion |

### Step 2: Apply the Five Assessment Questions

1. **"What am I not saying/doing because of how it would look?"** — Name the action you're avoiding and the appearance you're protecting. Is the protection worth the cost?

2. **"Am I optimizing for approval or outcomes?"** — What would maximize results? What would maximize approval? If they conflict, which am I choosing?

3. **"What would I do if no one was watching?"** — Remove social consequences. What's the right answer without reputation risk?

4. **"Am I being eloquent to avoid being decisive?"** — Tsunetomo: "Scholars hide their cowardice with wit and speech." Is your analysis sophisticated avoidance?

5. **"What's my 'IBM' in this situation?"** — What's the safe choice I can't be blamed for? What's the "La Quinta" — the better choice that looks risky?

### Step 3: The Appearance vs Substance Matrix

```
                    LOOKS GOOD
                    |
         Safe/      |      Ideal
         Mediocre   |      Rare
                    |
    ----------------+---------------- IS RIGHT
                    |
         Terrible   |      Requires
         Choice     |      Courage
                    |
                    LOOKS BAD
```

Most people unconsciously migrate toward "Looks Good" regardless of "Is Right."

### Step 4: Design the Courage

If social pressure is compromising your choice:

```
The "looking good" choice I'm tempted by: [describe]
The "being right" choice I'm avoiding: [describe]
The feared social consequence: [be specific]
Reality check: How likely? How recoverable?
My commitment: I will [being right choice] even though [feared consequence]
```

## Key Rules

- Surface the specific social calculation, the gap between "looks good" and "is right," and the real cost of choosing appearance
- Always apply the IBM trap check and "what if no one was watching" test
- Distinguish legitimate social consideration (relationships matter) from social pressure preventing right action
- Don't validate "being practical" when it means being conventional, or "being professional" when it means suppressing right action
- Fear of ostracism is deeply human — acknowledge it, but don't let it decide

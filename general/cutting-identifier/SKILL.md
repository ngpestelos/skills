# Cutting Identifier

Help identify the "cutting" — the single decisive action that determines success in a given domain. Based on Musashi's sword philosophy and Lynch's investing focus, synthesized by Sebastian Marshall.

## Core Principle

**Musashi**: "The primary thing when you take a sword in your hands is your intention to cut the enemy, whatever the means."

**Lynch**: Focus on earnings and assets; "What the stock price does today, tomorrow, or next week is only a distraction."

**The Pattern**: Masters identify ONE decisive thing and drive at it relentlessly, ignoring everything else.

## The Cutting Identification Framework

### Step 1: Domain Clarification

Ask: "What domain are we identifying cutting for?" (Professional role, project, life area, skill development)

### Step 2: Outcome Analysis

Four key questions:

1. **"What single outcome determines success here?"** — Not multiple outcomes. ONE. If you achieved this and nothing else, would you succeed?
2. **"What do masters in this domain obsess over?"** — Find your domain's Musashi and Lynch. What do they focus on that others ignore?
3. **"What can you ignore without affecting outcomes?"** — Everything else is noise.
4. **"If you had only one hour per day for this domain, what would you do?"** — Scarcity reveals essence.

### Step 3: Cutting Statement

Format: **"My cutting in [domain] is [specific action/focus]."**

| Domain | Cutting Example |
|--------|-----------------|
| Software Development | Ship value to users |
| Investing (Lynch) | Find companies with good earnings at good prices |
| Swordsmanship (Musashi) | Cut the enemy |
| Parenting | Be present and engaged during key moments |
| Knowledge Management | Capture and express ideas that compound |
| Career Strategy | Deliver exceptional value in one specific area |

### Step 4: Non-Cutting Elimination

List what your cutting is NOT:
- What seems important but doesn't determine outcomes?
- What are you doing because others do it?
- What feels productive but doesn't drive results?

## Key Rules

- There is only one cutting — never accept multiple
- Cutting must be outcome-based, not activity-based ("be excellent" and "work hard" are not cuttings)
- Emphasize singular focus over multiple priorities, outcomes over activities, masters' obsessions over common wisdom
- Don't reinforce "everything is important" — that's prioritization avoidance
